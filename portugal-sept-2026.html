Health & Entry
Medications & Entry Requirements
✅ Common OTC Meds — All Fine to Bring

These are unrestricted. Personal-use quantities, no documentation needed:

  • Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) — OTC in Portugal too
  • Loperamide (Imodium) — OTC, no issues
  • Acetaminophen/paracetamol (Tylenol) — OTC everywhere
  • Diphenhydramine (Benadryl, ZzzQuil, Unisom) — fine to bring
  • Bismuth subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol) — fine
  • Melatonin — great for jet lag, no restrictions

Tip: Portugal pharmacies (farmácias) stock most of these anyway, often cheaper than the US. Look for the green cross sign.

⚠️ Meloxicam (Mobic) — Prescription NSAID, Fine to Bring

Meloxicam is prescription-only in the EU — same as in the US. It is not a controlled substance. No special documentation required. Just bring it in the original Rx bottle with your name, prescribing doctor, and dosage on the label. No customs concerns.

⚠️ Sudafed (Pseudoephedrine) — Keep Original US Packaging

Pseudoephedrine is technically prescription-only in Portugal and the EU due to its use as a meth precursor. In practice, customs enforcement on personal travel amounts is essentially zero. Keep it in the original US box — do not decant into a pill organizer. Bringing a personal supply for an 8-day trip is not a real risk, but being caught with unmarked pills is asking for questions.

⚠️ DayQuil / NyQuil / Products Containing Dextromethorphan (DXM)

DXM is controlled in some EU countries. Portugal is relatively relaxed, but the same rule applies: original packaging only, personal amounts, clearly labeled. If your cold medicine contains DXM, make sure the product box comes with you — not just loose pills.

⚠️ THC / Cannabis

Portugal decriminalized personal drug use in 2001, but that applies to possession within Portugal — bringing THC products from the US is illegal and a customs offense. Do not travel internationally with any cannabis products, even from a legal US state. Leave it home.

⚠️ Adderall / Amphetamines

Adderall is not approved for use in Portugal and is classified as a controlled substance. If anyone in the group takes Adderall, carry the original prescription bottle, a letter from your doctor on letterhead, and keep amounts consistent with personal use only. Technically there can be complications at customs — carry documentation.

✅ Vaccines

No specific vaccines are required to enter Portugal from the US. Routine vaccines (COVID up to date, hepatitis A/B, tetanus) are recommended as general travel practice. No malaria risk in Portugal.

✅ No ETIAS Required for Your Trip

The EU's new ETIAS pre-travel authorization (like the US ESTA) is expected to launch Q4 2026 with a 6-month grace period — won't be required until ~April 2027. September 2026 is clear.

🛂 EES — Active at the Border

The EU Entry/Exit System launched April 2026. You'll give fingerprints and a face scan at Faro passport control on arrival. Nothing to prepare — happens automatically. Allow extra time in the immigration queue.

Packing & Practical
Weather, Packing & Practical Tips
☀️ September Weather

Algarve (Lagos/Faro): 82–90°F days, 65–70°F evenings. Virtually zero rain. Low humidity. Strong sun — sunscreen is not optional.
Lisbon: Slightly cooler — 75–85°F days, 62–68°F evenings. Coastal breeze. An occasional light rain is possible but unlikely. Evenings can feel genuinely cool — a layer is useful.

👗 How Locals Dress

Portuguese dress well — they don't tend to wear resort wear outside of the actual beach/pool. In Lisbon especially, smart casual is the standard. In Lagos it's more relaxed. Avoid wearing swimwear into restaurants or shops — throw on a cover-up or change first.

🔌 Power Adapters — Type F Plug Required

Portugal uses Type C/F plugs (same as most of mainland Europe). Voltage is 230V/50Hz — US is 110V/60Hz. Most modern devices (phones, laptops, cameras) are dual voltage — just check the label on your charger for "100-240V." If it says that, you only need an adapter (not a converter). Hair tools are often NOT dual voltage — check before you pack yours.
Shop adapters on Amazon →

💇 Hair Dryers

Most hotels provide hair dryers. Guesthouses and boutique properties (like Casa Mãe) typically do too — but worth confirming at booking if it's important. Don't bring a US hair dryer without a voltage converter — it will burn out immediately.

🏖 Towels at the Beach

Portuguese beaches do not provide towels — you bring your own or rent a sun lounger (which comes with a towel in some cases). If you're staying at a hotel like Carvi Beach Hotel or Hotel Faro, ask at check-in whether you can take hotel towels to the beach — most allow it. Otherwise, a compact travel towel is a good backup to pack.

💧 Tap Water

Tap water is safe to drink throughout Portugal. Bring a reusable water bottle — it saves money and the Algarve heat means you'll be drinking a lot of water.

💰 Cash vs. Card

Cards are accepted almost everywhere. However, a few places on this itinerary are cash only: Casinha do Petisco (Lagos) and Taberna da Rua das Flores (Lisbon). Bring some euro cash — ATMs are widely available and give the best exchange rates. Avoid airport currency exchange booths.

🎒 Carry-On Packing Tips

• Linen and cotton only — synthetics are miserable in 90°F heat
• Layers for Lisbon evenings — a linen button-down or light scarf does the job
• Comfortable walking shoes are non-negotiable — Lisbon's cobblestones are steep and slippery. White sneakers that look good are your best friend
• Small crossbody bag for daytime in Lisbon — keeps hands free and harder to pickpocket than a tote
• Sunscreen — the Algarve sun is intense, bring more than you think you need
• Portable charger — long beach and sightseeing days drain your phone
• Scarf — doubles as church coverage, beach wrap, and Lisbon evening layer

World Awareness
⚠️ Portugal Transport Strike — June 2026

Portugal experienced a major nationwide transport strike in June 2026, affecting trains, metro, flights, and public transport. Labor unrest around working condition reforms has been ongoing. Check for any planned strikes in August/September before you travel — particularly for CP train routes and TAP/connecting airlines. theportugalnews.com is a good English-language source to monitor.

🏳️‍🌈 LGBTQ+ Safety

Portugal is one of the most progressive countries in Europe on LGBTQ+ rights — consistently ranked in the top 5 in Europe. No concerns for any traveler.

📱 US Social Media Screening (Return)

When returning to the US, Customs and Border Protection has expanded social media screening programs. This is a US re-entry policy, not a Portugal issue, but worth being aware of before your return.

🏠 Overtourism Awareness

Lisbon has seen growing local frustration with mass tourism's impact on housing costs. Be a respectful traveler — shop local, eat at local spots (which we've already built into the itinerary), and be mindful of noise in residential neighborhoods.

Native Language
Language

Portuguese (Português) is the official language. The good news: English is widely spoken in tourist areas, especially in Lisbon, Lagos, and anywhere near the coast. Younger Portuguese people (under ~45) are generally comfortable in English. In smaller towns, rural areas, and older generations you'll encounter less English — but even then, a smile and a few Portuguese words go a long way.

Note: Portuguese and Spanish are related but not interchangeable. Don't assume a Portuguese speaker understands your Spanish — some do, but it's not a given and can feel dismissive to try.

Etiquette
Culture & Etiquette
🍞
The Couvert Charge — Read This First
When you sit down at a Portuguese restaurant, the waiter often places bread, olives, butter, or small appetizers on the table without being asked. These are not free. They'll appear on your bill as "couvert." If you don't want them, push them back or say "Não, obrigada." Once you eat them, you're paying for them — typically €1–4/person.
🕐
Meal Times Are Different
Lunch (1–3pm) is the main meal of the day. Dinner typically starts at 8–10pm. If you show up at a restaurant at 6pm you may find it closed or half-empty. Plan accordingly, especially in Lisbon.
⛪
Dress for Churches
Covered shoulders and no shorts required at Mosteiro dos Jerónimos, Igreja de Santo António (Lagos), and Faro Cathedral. Your linen scarf handles this — keep it accessible.
🤫
Portuguese Are Reserved, Not Rude
Portuguese culture is warm but not outwardly exuberant. Don't mistake quiet politeness for coldness. Greet with "Olá" or "Bom dia" when entering any shop, café, or restaurant — it matters.
🌊
Saudade
There's a beautiful, untranslatable Portuguese word — saudade (sow-DAH-day) — meaning a deep nostalgic longing for something or someone. It's central to Portuguese soul and why fado music exists. You'll feel it when you're there.
🍺
Drinking Outdoors — Open Container Is Fine
Portugal has no open container laws. Drinking a beer or wine outside — on a plaza, at a miradouro, on a bench — is completely normal and you'll see locals doing it. Picking up a cold Super Bock from a mini-market and drinking it outside while you watch the sunset is a legitimate evening activity.

Walking around with drinks is less of a cultural habit (Portuguese tend to sit and linger), but nobody will bat an eye at a water bottle or beer in hand on a hot day. The one thing that reads as tourist: the paper takeaway coffee cup while walking — but even that isn't rude, just uncommon. What to avoid is being visibly sloppy — Portuguese drinking culture is relaxed, not rowdy.
🏳️‍🌈
LGBTQ+
Portugal is one of the most LGBTQ+-friendly countries in Europe. Same-sex marriage has been legal since 2010. Lisbon in particular is very open and welcoming. No concerns for anyone in the group.
Transport
Getting Around
🚂 Trains (CP)

Intercity travel is all on CP (Comboios de Portugal). Clean, punctual, affordable. Book ahead for the Alfa Pendular routes. cp.pt →

🚗 Rideshare: Bolt > Uber in Portugal

Download Bolt — it's the dominant rideshare app in Portugal, typically 20–30% cheaper than Uber and drivers prefer it. Uber works too, especially in Lisbon, but Bolt is the local standard. Both operate in Lisbon and the Algarve. bolt.eu →

🚃 Lisbon Trams

Tram 15E (to Belém) and Tram 28 (Alfama) are iconic but notorious for pickpockets. Keep bags in front of you, hands on your phone. The 15E is worth it for the Belém ride — it's scenic. The 28 is beautiful but can be skipped in favor of a taxi/Bolt if security is a concern.

🚇 Lisbon Metro

The metro is easy, clean, and cheap. Buy a rechargeable Viva Viagem card (€0.50) at any station and load trips onto it. Far better value than single tickets. metrolisboa.pt →

🛺 Tuk-Tuks (Lisbon Only)

Electric tuk-tuks are a Lisbon staple — best for exploring hilly neighborhoods like Alfama, Mouraria, and Bairro Alto where walking uphill gets old fast. They're more sightseeing than transit: most operate as 1–2 hour neighborhood tours. Book in advance on GetYourGuide or Viator, or find driver stands near Praça do Comércio and Alfama. Expect €15–30/person depending on tour length. Not practical for cross-city trips — use Bolt for that.

🚕 Lagos & Algarve

Lagos is very walkable for beaches and Old Town. Bolt/Uber available for the short trips (Casa Chico Zé, Meia Praia). No public transit network worth using in the Algarve — taxis or rideshare is the move.

Food Culture
Food — What to Expect
🐟
Seafood Is Everywhere — and That's a Good Thing
Portugal has one of the highest seafood consumption rates in Europe. The Atlantic is right there, and menus reflect it. Fish, shrimp, octopus, and crab are staples — and incredibly fresh. If you don't eat seafood, you'll still eat well (meat dishes are strong), but don't be surprised if seafood dominates most menus.
🐠
Fish Is Often Served Whole — Head On
In traditional restaurants, grilled fish (Dourada/sea bream, Robalo/sea bass, sardines) typically arrives whole with the head on. This is completely normal and means the fish is fresh. You eat around the bones. If this bothers anyone in the group, it's totally fine to ask "sem cabeça" (without the head) — most kitchens will accommodate.
🥫
Tinned Fish (Conservas) — Not What You Think
Portugal has an artisanal tinned fish culture that is nothing like American canned fish. Beautifully packaged tins of sardines, mackerel, tuna, octopus, and cod in olive oil are considered a delicacy — often eaten on crusty bread with wine. There are entire shops dedicated to conservas in Lisbon (look in Chiado and Alfama). They make excellent, packable souvenirs. One popular shop: Conserveira de Lisboa.
🧂
Bacalhau — Salted Cod
Portugal's most iconic ingredient. Bacalhau is cod that's been dried and salt-cured — then reconstituted and cooked. It looks and tastes nothing like fresh fish. It's intensely salty, dense, and has a very particular flavor. It's said Portugal has 365 recipes for it (one per day of the year). If you're curious, try it. If the smell puts you off, just steer toward the fresh fish dishes instead.
🫒
No Butter With Bread
Bread in Portugal is typically served with olive oil, not butter. In many restaurants, small olives, cheese, or charcuterie also come alongside. Just remember — see the couvert note above. These are not free once you eat them.
🍽
Portions Are Generous — "Meia Dose" Is Your Friend
Portuguese restaurant portions are often enormous. Many traditional restaurants offer a meia dose (half portion) for roughly 60–70% of the full price. If you're ordering multiple dishes or tapas-style, ask first — it can save food waste and money.
🌱
Vegetarian Options Are Limited in Traditional Spots
Portuguese cuisine is heavily meat and seafood based. Modern Lisbon has excellent vegetarian and vegan restaurants, but traditional tascos and grills in Lagos and Faro offer limited options beyond salad, eggs, or cheese. If anyone in the group is vegetarian, seek out newer spots in Lisbon and confirm options when calling ahead at traditional restaurants.
☕
Milk Alternatives — Patchy Outside Lisbon
Oat milk (leite de aveia), almond milk (leite de amêndoa), and soy (leite de soja) are available in modern specialty coffee shops in Lisbon — look for places with flat whites and lattes on the menu. In traditional pastelarias, smaller Lagos cafés, and most Faro spots, don't count on it. Ask: "Tem leite de aveia?" (Do you have oat milk?) Supermarkets stock them widely if you want to pick some up.
☕ Portuguese Coffee Guide — For the Coffee People

Portuguese coffee is strong, cheap, and served in tiny ceramic cups. It is nothing like American drip coffee. Here's what you'll see on menus:

Bica / Café
BEE-kah / kah-FAY
Espresso shot. "Bica" in Lisbon, "cimbalino" in Porto, "café" everywhere. The standard. Strong, short, served in a ceramic cup. €0.65–1.00 at the counter.
Café Duplo
kah-FAY DOO-ploh
Double espresso. Two shots in a standard cup.
Abatanado
ah-bah-tah-NAH-doh
Espresso with extra hot water — the closest thing to an Americano. Served in a larger cup. Good if you find espresso too intense.
Garoto
gah-ROH-toh
Espresso with just a small splash of milk — like a macchiato. Served in an espresso cup. Literally means "little boy." Great middle ground between black and milky.
Meia de Leite
MAY-ah deh LAY-teh
Half coffee, half steamed milk in a medium glass. Closest to an American latte. Typically a morning drink — ordering at night may get a look.
Galão ⭐ Try This
gah-LOWNG
¼ espresso + ¾ foamy milk, served in a tall glass. Portugal's most iconic coffee. Similar to a large latte. Beautiful to look at. ~€1.20–1.80. Morning drink.
Descafeinado
desh-kah-fay-NAH-doh
Decaf espresso. Available at most places — just ask.
Café com Gelo
kah-FAY com ZHEH-loh
"Iced coffee" — but not really. They bring a hot espresso and a glass of ice. You pour it yourself. It works but it's not cold brew. Modern Lisbon shops increasingly offer proper iced lattes.
💡 Counter vs. Table Pricing
Standing at the counter (ao balcão) is cheaper than sitting at a table (à mesa), which is cheaper than sitting outside (na esplanada). A €0.80 espresso at the counter might be €1.20 at a table and €1.50 outside. The price difference is usually posted on the menu — look for three columns. It's not a trick, it's just how it works.
💡 Seating Etiquette — Sit First, Then Order
At any café or restaurant with table seating — indoors or outside — sit down first and wait for the waiter to come to you. This is always table service. Don't go up to the counter to order if there are tables. Standing at the counter is a separate, self-service experience. One caveat: at outdoor spots in touristy areas the waiter may take a few minutes to acknowledge you — that's normal, they'll get to you. Just make eye contact when you can.
💡 Takeaway Is Not the Default
Walking around with a paper cup of coffee is not a thing in traditional Portuguese culture. Most pastelarias don't stock paper cups. If you want to take it with you, say "para levar, por favor" (to take away) — a modern café may accommodate, a traditional one may just hand you a ceramic cup and assume you'll drink it at the counter. Plan to stand and enjoy it rather than walk with it.
💧
Still vs. Sparkling Water
In restaurants you'll often be asked "com gás ou sem gás?" (sparkling or still?). Sparkling (com gás) is popular in Portugal — don't be surprised if it arrives automatically. If you want still, say "sem gás, por favor."
🍷
Wine Is Remarkably Cheap and Good
A glass of house wine in a local restaurant might be €2–4. A full bottle of excellent local wine: €10–20. Portuguese wines are genuinely world-class and underpriced internationally. Vinho verde (slightly sparkling, crisp white) is perfect for the summer heat. If you're a wine drinker, this is a very happy country to be in.
🍒
Ginjinha — Try It
A sour cherry liqueur sold for €1–2 a shot from tiny kiosks in Lisbon's Alfama. It's Portugal's unofficial shot and a genuine local experience. You'll find vendors near Largo de São Domingos in Lisbon. Served in a tiny ceramic cup, sometimes with a preserved cherry at the bottom.
🥂 Drinks Guide — What to Order

From regional spirits to wine to non-alcoholic options — what's worth trying and what to ask for.

🌿 Medronho — The Algarve Spirit
This is the local spirit for the region you're visiting. Made from the medronho berry (strawberry tree fruit) — a clear, strong, slightly fruity digestif. Traditional Algarve restaurants often bring a small glass free at the end of a meal without being asked. Sip it, don't shoot it.
🍷 Vinho Verde
Portugal's most refreshing wine — young, slightly sparkling, crisp, low alcohol (8–11%). Named "green wine" because it's bottled young, not because of the color. White vinho verde is perfect for the Algarve heat. Usually under €15 for a bottle.
🍷 Algarve Wines
The Algarve now has a growing local wine scene — look for regional wines on menus (often just labeled "vinho regional Algarve"). They tend to be bold reds and fruity whites that pair well with grilled fish and meat. Excellent value.
🍺 Beer — Super Bock vs. Sagres
Portugal's two national beers. Super Bock (from Porto) and Sagres (from Lisbon) are the default lagers everywhere. Light, cold, and perfect with seafood. Ask for "uma imperial" (draft, ~25cl) or "uma caneca" (larger draft). Super Bock is generally the better beer.
ℹ️ What About Poncha?
You may have seen Poncha mentioned in Portugal content — it's actually a Madeira island specialty (aguardente + honey + lemon), not a mainland drink. Some tourist bars in the Algarve now serve it because it's become widely known, so you might see it on a menu. If you do, it's worth trying — but it's not a local tradition for where you're going.
🌿 Non-Alcoholic Options — For the Non-Drinker
Sumol — Portugal's beloved fruit soda. Passionfruit (maracujá) is the iconic flavor. Widely available everywhere, feels local and intentional rather than a "I don't drink" order.

Sumo natural — fresh-squeezed orange juice, pressed to order at most cafés. Excellent and cheap (~€2–3).

Água com gás e limão — sparkling water with lemon. The go-to elegant non-alcoholic order at dinner or a bar. Nobody blinks.

Mocktails — Modern cocktail bars in Lisbon increasingly offer dedicated mocktail menus. In Lagos, hit or miss — a good bar will accommodate if asked ("cocktail sem álcool, por favor"). Don't expect it at traditional restaurants.

O%riginal — a Portuguese brand making 0% wine (white, red, rosé) that's widely available in supermarkets if she wants to match the table at dinner.

Docs & Border Info

🛂 EES — Active & Affects Your Trip

Launched April 10, 2026. On arrival at FAO, all 3 travelers submit fingerprints + facial scan at passport control. Nothing to prep — happens automatically. Allow extra queue time. Check all 3 passports are valid through March 8, 2027.

✅ ETIAS — Not Required for September 2026

EU pre-travel authorization launches Q4 2026, won't be mandatory until ~April 2027. No action needed for this trip.

Visa
Not required for US passport holders (Schengen, 90 days)
Currency
Euro. Cards widely accepted. Bring cash for Casinha do Petisco + Taberna da Rua das Flores (cash only).
Tipping
Not expected. Rounding up or €1–2 appreciated.

Uber works in Lisbon + major Algarve towns.
