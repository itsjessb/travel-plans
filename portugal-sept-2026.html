Health
Medications & What to Pack
✅ Common OTC Meds — All Fine to Bring

These are unrestricted. Personal-use quantities, no documentation needed:

  • Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) — OTC in Portugal too
  • Loperamide (Imodium) — OTC, no issues
  • Acetaminophen/paracetamol (Tylenol) — OTC everywhere
  • Diphenhydramine (Benadryl, ZzzQuil, Unisom) — fine to bring
  • Bismuth subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol) — fine
  • Melatonin — great for jet lag, no restrictions

Tip: Portugal pharmacies (farmácias) stock most of these anyway, often cheaper than the US. Look for the green cross sign.

⚠️ Meloxicam (Mobic) — Prescription NSAID, Fine to Bring

Meloxicam is prescription-only in the EU — same as in the US. It is not a controlled substance. No special documentation required. Just bring it in the original Rx bottle with your name, prescribing doctor, and dosage on the label.

⚠️ Sudafed (Pseudoephedrine) — Keep Original US Packaging

Pseudoephedrine is technically prescription-only in Portugal and the EU due to its use as a meth precursor. In practice, customs enforcement on personal travel amounts is essentially zero. Keep it in the original US box — do not decant into a pill organizer.

⚠️ DayQuil / NyQuil / Products Containing Dextromethorphan (DXM)

DXM is controlled in some EU countries. Portugal is relatively relaxed, but the same rule applies: original packaging only, personal amounts, clearly labeled.

⚠️ THC / Cannabis

Portugal decriminalized personal drug use in 2001, but that applies to possession within Portugal — bringing THC products from the US is illegal and a customs offense. Do not travel internationally with any cannabis products.

⚠️ Adderall / Amphetamines

Adderall is not approved for use in Portugal and is classified as a controlled substance. If anyone in the group takes Adderall, carry the original prescription bottle, a letter from your doctor on letterhead, and keep amounts consistent with personal use only.

✅ Vaccines

No specific vaccines are required to enter Portugal from the US. Routine vaccines (COVID up to date, hepatitis A/B, tetanus) are recommended as general travel practice. No malaria risk in Portugal.

Packing & Practical
Weather, Packing & Practical Tips
☀️ September Weather

Algarve (Lagos/Faro): 82–90°F days, 65–70°F evenings. Virtually zero rain. Low humidity. Strong sun — sunscreen is not optional.
Lisbon: Slightly cooler — 75–85°F days, 62–68°F evenings. Coastal breeze. An occasional light rain is possible but unlikely. Evenings can feel genuinely cool — a layer is useful.

👗 How Locals Dress

Portuguese dress well — they don't tend to wear resort wear outside of the actual beach/pool. In Lisbon especially, smart casual is the standard. In Lagos it's more relaxed. Avoid wearing swimwear into restaurants or shops.

🔌 Power Adapters — Type F Plug Required

Portugal uses Type C/F plugs. Voltage is 230V/50Hz. Most modern devices (phones, laptops, cameras) are dual voltage — check the label for "100-240V." If it says that, you only need an adapter. Hair tools are often NOT dual voltage — check before you pack.
Shop adapters on Amazon →

💇 Hair Dryers

Most hotels provide hair dryers. Don't bring a US hair dryer without a voltage converter — it will burn out immediately.

🏖 Towels at the Beach

Portuguese beaches do not provide towels — you bring your own or rent a sun lounger. If staying at Carvi or Hotel Faro, ask at check-in whether you can take hotel towels to the beach.

💧 Tap Water

Tap water is safe to drink throughout Portugal. Bring a reusable water bottle — the Algarve heat means you'll be drinking a lot.

💰 Cash vs. Card

Cards are accepted almost everywhere. However, two places on this itinerary are cash only: Casinha do Petisco (Lagos) and Taberna da Rua das Flores (Lisbon). Bring some euro cash — ATMs give the best exchange rates.

🎒 Carry-On Packing Tips

• Linen and cotton only — synthetics are miserable in 90°F heat
• Layers for Lisbon evenings — a linen button-down or light scarf
• Comfortable walking shoes are non-negotiable — Lisbon's cobblestones are steep and slippery
• Small crossbody bag for daytime in Lisbon — harder to pickpocket than a tote
• Sunscreen — the Algarve sun is intense, bring more than you think you need
• Portable charger — long beach and sightseeing days drain your phone
• Scarf — doubles as church coverage, beach wrap, and Lisbon evening layer

🏳️‍🌈 LGBTQ+ Safety

Portugal is one of the most progressive countries in Europe on LGBTQ+ rights — consistently ranked in the top 5 in Europe. No concerns for any traveler.

📱 US Social Media Screening (Return)

When returning to the US, Customs and Border Protection has expanded social media screening programs. This is a US re-entry policy, not a Portugal issue, but worth being aware of before your return.

🏠 Overtourism Awareness

Lisbon has seen growing local frustration with mass tourism's impact on housing costs. Be a respectful traveler — shop local, eat at local spots (already built into the itinerary), and be mindful of noise in residential neighborhoods.

Valid US passport required. Must be valid for at least 3 months beyond your departure date (Sept 8). Check expiry now.
Visa
No visa required for US citizens staying up to 90 days in the Schengen Area.
Travel Insurance
Strongly recommended. Covers trip cancellation, medical emergencies, and lost luggage. Check your credit card benefits first.

Most US health plans don't cover international emergencies. Travel insurance with medical coverage or a dedicated travel medical policy is recommended.

🛂 EES — EU Entry/Exit System (Active April 2026)

At Faro passport control on arrival you will be asked to give fingerprints and a face scan as part of the EU's new Entry/Exit System. This is automatic — nothing to prepare in advance. Expect queues to be longer than usual as border agents process the biometric scans. Allow at least 30–45 extra minutes in your immigration estimate. On departure, the same scan applies at the gate.

✅ ETIAS — Not Required for September 2026

The EU's new ETIAS pre-travel authorization (similar to the US ESTA) is expected to launch Q4 2026 with a 6-month grace period. It will not be required before ~April 2027. Your trip in September 2026 is in the clear — no action needed.

Native Language
Language

Portuguese (Português) is the official language. English is widely spoken in tourist areas, especially in Lisbon, Lagos, and anywhere near the coast. Younger Portuguese people (under ~45) are generally comfortable in English.

Note: Portuguese and Spanish are related but not interchangeable. Don't assume a Portuguese speaker understands your Spanish — some do, but it's not a given and can feel dismissive.

Etiquette
Culture & Etiquette
🍞
The Couvert Charge — Read This First
When you sit down at a Portuguese restaurant, the waiter often places bread, olives, butter, or small appetizers on the table without being asked. These are not free. They'll appear on your bill as "couvert." If you don't want them, push them back or say "Não, obrigada." Once you eat them, you're paying for them — typically €1–4/person.
🕐
Meal Times Are Different
Lunch (1–3pm) is the main meal of the day. Dinner typically starts at 8–10pm. If you show up at a restaurant at 6pm you may find it closed or half-empty.
⛪
Dress for Churches
Covered shoulders and no shorts required at Mosteiro dos Jerónimos, Igreja de Santo António (Lagos), and Faro Cathedral. Your linen scarf handles this.
🤫
Portuguese Are Reserved, Not Rude
Portuguese culture is warm but not outwardly exuberant. Greet with "Olá" or "Bom dia" when entering any shop, café, or restaurant — it matters.
🌊
Saudade
There's a beautiful, untranslatable Portuguese word — saudade (sow-DAH-day) — meaning a deep nostalgic longing for something or someone. It's central to Portuguese soul and why fado music exists. You'll feel it when you're there.
🍺
Drinking Outdoors — Open Container Is Fine
Portugal has no open container laws. Drinking a beer or wine outside — on a plaza, at a miradouro, on a bench — is completely normal and you'll see locals doing it. What to avoid is being visibly sloppy — Portuguese drinking culture is relaxed, not rowdy.
🏳️‍🌈
LGBTQ+
Portugal is one of the most LGBTQ+-friendly countries in Europe. Same-sex marriage has been legal since 2010. Lisbon in particular is very open and welcoming. No concerns for anyone in the group.
Transport
Getting Around
🚂 Trains (CP)

Intercity travel is all on CP (Comboios de Portugal). Clean, punctual, affordable. Book ahead for the Alfa Pendular routes. cp.pt →

💶 Estimated cost: Lagos → Lisbon (Alfa Pendular) ~€20–30/person. Regional trains cheaper but slower.

🚗 Rideshare: Bolt > Uber in Portugal

Download Bolt — it's the dominant rideshare app in Portugal, typically 20–30% cheaper than Uber. Both operate in Lisbon and the Algarve. bolt.eu →

💶 Estimated cost: Short city trips €4–10. Airport → city center €15–25. Lagos → Faro Airport ~€35–50.

🚃 Lisbon Trams

Tram 15E (to Belém) and Tram 28 (Alfama) are iconic but notorious for pickpockets. Keep bags in front of you, hands on your phone.

💶 Estimated cost: ~€3.00/trip with Viva Viagem card (more expensive if bought on the tram).

🚇 Lisbon Metro

The metro is easy, clean, and cheap. Buy a rechargeable Viva Viagem card (€0.50) at any station and load trips onto it. metrolisboa.pt →

💶 Estimated cost: €1.61/trip. 24-hour pass ~€6.60 — worth it if you're doing 4+ rides in a day.

🛺 Tuk-Tuks (Lisbon Only)

Electric tuk-tuks are a Lisbon staple — best for exploring hilly neighborhoods like Alfama, Mouraria, and Bairro Alto. Most operate as 1–2 hour neighborhood tours. Book on GetYourGuide or Viator, or find stands near Praça do Comércio and Alfama. Expect €15–30/person.

🚕 Lagos & Algarve

Lagos is very walkable for beaches and Old Town. Bolt/Uber available for short trips. No public transit network worth using in the Algarve.

💶 Estimated cost: Most Lagos trips €4–8. Beach to town €5–10. Longer Algarve day trips €20–40.

Food Culture
Food — What to Expect
🐟
Seafood Is Everywhere
Portugal has one of the highest seafood consumption rates in Europe. Fish, shrimp, octopus, and crab are staples — and incredibly fresh. If you don't eat seafood, you'll still eat well, but don't be surprised if seafood dominates most menus.
🐠
Fish Is Often Served Whole — Head On
In traditional restaurants, grilled fish typically arrives whole with the head on. This is completely normal and means the fish is fresh. It's totally fine to ask "sem cabeça" (without the head).
🥫
Tinned Fish (Conservas) — Not What You Think
Portugal has an artisanal tinned fish culture — beautifully packaged tins of sardines, mackerel, tuna in olive oil are considered a delicacy. They make excellent, packable souvenirs.
🫒
No Butter With Bread
Bread in Portugal is typically served with olive oil, not butter. Remember — see the couvert note above. These are not free once you eat them.
🍽
Portions Are Generous — "Meia Dose" Is Your Friend
Portuguese restaurant portions are often enormous. Many traditional restaurants offer a meia dose (half portion) for roughly 60–70% of the full price.
🌱
Vegetarian Options Are Limited in Traditional Spots
Modern Lisbon has excellent vegetarian and vegan restaurants, but traditional spots in Lagos and Faro offer limited options beyond salad, eggs, or cheese.
☕ Portuguese Coffee Guide

Portuguese coffee is strong, cheap, and served in tiny ceramic cups. Here's what you'll see on menus:

Bica / Café
BEE-kah / kah-FAY
Espresso shot. "Bica" in Lisbon, "café" everywhere. Strong, short. €0.65–1.00 at the counter.

gah-LOWNG
¼ espresso + ¾ foamy milk in a tall glass. Portugal's most iconic coffee. ~€1.20–1.80.
Abatanado
ah-bah-tah-NAH-doh
Espresso with extra hot water — closest to an Americano. Good if you find espresso too intense.
Meia de Leite
MAY-ah deh LAY-teh
Half coffee, half steamed milk. Closest to an American latte. Morning drink.
💡 Counter vs. Table Pricing
Standing at the counter is cheaper than a table, which is cheaper than outside. A €0.80 espresso at the counter might be €1.50 outside. Usually posted on the menu in three columns.
💡 Sit First, Then Order
At any café or restaurant with table seating — sit down and wait for the waiter. Don't go up to the counter to order if there are tables.
💧
Still vs. Sparkling Water
You'll often be asked "com gás ou sem gás?". Sparkling is popular — if you want still, say "sem gás, por favor."
🍷
Wine Is Remarkably Cheap and Good
A glass of house wine might be €2–4. A full bottle of excellent local wine: €10–20. Vinho verde (slightly sparkling, crisp white) is perfect for the summer heat.
🍒
Ginjinha — Try It
A sour cherry liqueur sold for €1–2 a shot from tiny kiosks in Lisbon's Alfama. Portugal's unofficial shot. Served in a tiny ceramic cup, sometimes with a preserved cherry at the bottom.
🥂 Drinks Guide
🌿 Medronho — The Algarve Spirit
Made from the medronho berry — a clear, strong, slightly fruity digestif. Traditional Algarve restaurants often bring a small glass free at the end of a meal. Sip it, don't shoot it.
🍷 Vinho Verde
Young, slightly sparkling, crisp, low alcohol (8–11%). Named "green wine" because it's bottled young. Perfect for the Algarve heat. Usually under €15 for a bottle.
🍺 Beer — Super Bock vs. Sagres
Portugal's two national beers. Ask for "uma imperial" (draft, ~25cl) or "uma caneca" (larger draft). Super Bock is generally the better beer.
🌿 Non-Alcoholic Options
Sumol — Portugal's beloved fruit soda. Passionfruit (maracujá) is the iconic flavor.

Sumo natural — fresh-squeezed orange juice, pressed to order. Excellent and cheap (~€2–3).

Água com gás e limão — sparkling water with lemon. The go-to elegant non-alcoholic order at dinner or a bar.

O%riginal — a Portuguese brand making 0% wine widely available in supermarkets.

💡 Spend More at Lunch Than Dinner
Portuguese restaurant culture front-loads the day. The prato do dia (daily special) at lunch is typically the same dishes offered at dinner for a third of the price — often includes bread, a drink, and dessert. Save the big dinner budget for special occasions.
💡 Tipping
Not expected, but appreciated. Rounding up or leaving €2–5 on a full dinner is the norm. Tipping 15–20% as you would in the US is not standard and not necessary.

✅ Pros

Most Lisbon time of any option — 3.5 full days including a Cascais day trip. Strong Faro/beach finish. No car needed. Cheapest option at $1,871.50/pp total.

⚠️ Watch

No Lagos, no Algarve cliff beaches. Tram 28 is a pickpocket hotspot — bags in front. Sept 6 train timing (10am vs. 2pm) affects how much of the day is lost to travel.

My call: Best option for the group that wants to really dig into Lisbon. Four full days, a day trip to Cascais, the Clube de Fado, and a beach finish in Faro. Most affordable option by a meaningful margin.

Sept 3
Chiado — Food, Shopping, FadoThe Folks · Livraria Bertrand · Clube de Fado
Morning
Brunch at The Folks Chiado.
Afternoon — Shopping
Chiado: Josefinas (Portuguese leather flats), Salsa Jeans, largest Zara flagship on Rua do Carmo, vintage shops in Príncipe Real.
Must-stop
Livraria Bertrand — the oldest operating bookstore in the world (founded 1732). They stamp your book certifying you bought it here.
Evening
Dinner and live fado at Clube de Fado — one of Lisbon's most respected venues. Must make reservations.

04
Sept 4
Cascais Day TripDear Breakfast · 40-min train · Beach · Estoril promenade
Morning
Brunch at Dear Breakfast.
Mid-Morning
Train from Cais do Sodré → Cascais (~40 min, ~€3). Scenic coastal line — Atlantic the whole way.
Afternoon
Explore Cascais — old town, marina, local cafés, beach. Walk the flat waterfront promenade to Estoril (~2km, 20 min) — grand casino on one side, ocean on the other.
Evening
Train back to Lisbon. Dinner in Chiado or Bairro Alto — best bar strip in the city if anyone wants to keep the night going.
05
Sept 5
Tram 28 + ExploreBreakfast Lovers · Alfama · Graça · Baixa
8–9am
Breakfast at Breakfast Lovers — café right on the Tram 28 route. Hop directly onto Tram 28 — rides through Alfama, Graça, Baixa, and Chiado.
⚠️ Tram 28 pickpockets: Keep bags in front of you, phone in a front pocket. Don't put anything in a back pocket.
Rest of Day
Free Lisbon day — Miradouro de Santa Luzia, Miradouro da Graça, Alfama on foot. Anything from the shopping list you didn't get on Day 3.
