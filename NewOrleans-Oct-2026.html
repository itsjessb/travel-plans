Construction
French Quarter Infrastructure Work

⚠️ Active construction — may still be ongoing in October

The Sewerage & Water Board has been replacing underground water, sewer, and drainage pipes throughout the French Quarter since 2025. Primarily affecting Decatur Street and St. Peters Street, with construction fencing and torn-up pavement that has hurt foot traffic for local businesses. One stretch (Philip to Dumaine) had an estimated Fall 2026 completion, but there's no confirmed end date for the full project and it has already run behind schedule. As of April 2026 (French Quarter Fest), some disruption was still present.

What it means for you: Decatur and St. Peters may have sidewalk closures or construction fencing in October. Royal Street and Chartres Street (one block away, where most of your activity is) remain fully walkable. This doesn't change the itinerary — just be aware you may need to route around those two blocks.

Packing
What to Wear & What to Bring
👟 Shoes — This Is the Most Important Packing Note

The French Quarter's "banquettes" (brick sidewalks) are notoriously uneven — cracked, raised, and unpredictable underfoot. You will be walking miles. Heels are mostly reserved for destination moments (sitting at Commander's, arriving at a restaurant) — not for walking between spots. Comfortable and stylish is the NOLA standard. Bring at least one pair of genuinely supportive walking shoes. Your feet will thank you by Day 3.

🌤 Layers for Evenings

Days are warm (75–78°F) but evenings drop to the low-to-mid 60s. A light layer — linen jacket, cardigan, or scarf — is all you need. October nights can feel genuinely cool after a while, especially near the river.

👗 How NOLA Dresses

This is not a dress-up city. Smart casual is the standard for most dinners and bars. You rarely see heels and formal attire on the street — NOLA is about comfort and self-expression, not formality. The exception is Commander's Palace (business casual required) and a handful of upscale dining rooms. Pack stylish and comfortable — that combination fits everywhere.

🎒 Bag Strategy

A crossbody bag is ideal for daytime — it's secure, keeps your hands free, and works well with the walking pace of the Quarter. You'll want cash accessible (see Culture section). For evenings, a small clutch works. Magazine Street shopping days call for a tote.

☂️ Rain + Sun

A compact travel umbrella takes almost no space and is worth it with a 30% daily rain chance. Sunscreen too — even in October the southern sun has real UV. Don't skip it.

💵 Cash

Carry $40–60 in cash at all times. Many smaller bars, street readers, musicians, and specialty shops in NOLA are cash-preferred or cash-only. Street tarot readers on Jackson Square are cash only. ATMs in the Quarter charge high fees — pull cash from your bank before you leave Phoenix.

Culture & Local Knowledge
New Orleans Is Unlike Anywhere Else in the US
🤝 People Are Genuinely Friendly

Expect strangers to start conversations. It's not weird — it's normal here. Shop owners, bartenders, and people on the street will engage with you and mean it. Lean into it. Some of the best recommendations come from a 3-minute conversation at a bar.

🎵 Stop When You Hear Live Music

Live music is on the street corners, in the bars, in the restaurants, floating out of second-floor windows. NOLA produces world-class musicians who play for tips. If something sounds good, stop for a few minutes. This is one of the things that makes the city magic — don't walk past it to get somewhere faster.

🥁 Second Lines & Jazz Funerals

You may randomly encounter a jazz funeral or second line parade — a brass band leading a procession through the streets, with the "second line" of bystanders joining behind with handkerchiefs and dancing. Stop everything and join the second line. This is a gift if it happens.

🕐 NOLA Time

The city operates on a relaxed clock. Things run 10–15 minutes late. Services start when they start. This is not Phoenix or Chicago. Embrace it — trying to fight the pace is the wrong move. The city will reward you for slowing down.

🍹 You Can Drink on the Street

Open container laws in the French Quarter are extremely relaxed — you can walk out of a bar with a plastic cup drink. (Glass containers are technically prohibited outside.) This is why you'll see people casually strolling with drinks, and why the "to-go cup" is a NOLA institution. Frozen daiquiri shops are everywhere — this is a legal and beloved local tradition.

🏙️ The Neighborhoods Are Very Different

The French Quarter is touristy but historic. The Garden District (where Commander's is) is residential, gorgeous antebellum mansions, quieter. The Marigny/Frenchmen St (where JAMNOLA is) is the local music and arts district. Uptown/Magazine St is where locals shop and eat. Each is worth experiencing — they feel like different cities.

💰 Tipping

Be generous. Restaurant servers, bartenders, tour guides, street musicians (a dollar or two is fine), tarot readers. NOLA's hospitality industry runs on tips and the workers are generally excellent.

Food & Drinks
What to Eat & Drink While You're There
Gumbo
The city's signature dish. Dark, rich roux-based stew with seafood, chicken, or andouille sausage. Served over rice. Every restaurant has a version — they all taste different and none of them are wrong.
Jambalaya
Rice cooked with the Trinity (onion, celery, bell pepper) plus meat and/or seafood. Similar spirit to paella. Deeply comforting and filling.
Po'Boy
French bread sandwich — go for hot sausage, fried shrimp, or roast beef "dressed" (lettuce, tomato, pickles, mayo). One of NOLA's great street foods.
Muffaletta
Round Italian bread loaded with cured meats and olive salad. Napoleon House invented it and still makes it well. Order it at lunch.
Étouffée
Crawfish or shrimp smothered in a rich butter sauce. Served over rice. If you see crawfish étouffée on a menu, order it.
Redfish on the Half Shell
A NOLA specialty — redfish cooked skin-down on its own scales. Blistered, smoky, incredible. Look for it at Bayona or Sylvain if it's on the menu.
Beignets
Hot fried dough, buried in powdered sugar. Café Du Monde (Jackson Square, open 24/7) is the original. Go once, expect to wear the powdered sugar.
Bread Pudding
The classic NOLA dessert — old bread, custard, whiskey sauce. Every restaurant does it differently. Commander's Palace version is exceptional.
Drinks Worth Ordering
Sazerac
Rye whiskey, absinthe rinse, Peychaud's bitters. Invented at The Roosevelt (your hotel). Order one at the Sazerac Bar.
Vieux Carré
Cognac, rye, sweet vermouth, Bénédictine, bitters. Invented at the Carousel Bar (your Day 2 stop). Order it there.
Pimm's Cup
Light, cucumber, Pimm's liqueur. Napoleon House's signature. The most refreshing thing you'll drink in October heat.
Frozen Daiquiri (to-go)
Plastic cup, completely legal to walk with, genuinely delicious. Do it once — it's a NOLA institution. Avoid the neon-colored tourist shops; look for smaller spots with fresh fruit versions.
